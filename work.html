CASE STUDY

App Re-Design Concept

In a pandemic world, things can get pretty hectic, our workloads stacking and stacking. That‚Äôs why its important not to forget one‚Äôs own wellbeing. GoSlow is an app that works to create a space for oneself, where one can slow down and check in. My goal for the project was to provide a satisfying redesign of this app‚Äôs outdated interface in order to make it modern, and navigable for its users.

THE CHALLENGE

I brought in individuals on zoom, for an interview, where I showcased the old interface and asked for them to interact with it in Figma preview, noting anything that stuck out to them (the old version is incompatible with most current devices). I chose this method of information gathering as I was not only able to watch for the reactive expressions of my interviewees, but watch as they interacted with the older product through screen share.

DESIGN PROCESS

Since most users of GoSlow would be mobile users, its arguable that the mobile experience is heavily important for improving. In comparison with websites, native apps can supply a user with additional features and benefits. Since this app is intended for for busy users on the go, it‚Äôs important that the overall user experience provides a sense of calm and is easily-accessible. This is why I have decided to rely heavily on blue, as the color is proven psychologically to create sentiments of safety and calm.

DIARY SCREEN

I learned that the lack of whitespace on the suggestion screen caused quite of few of my interviewees to take more time understanding its visual hierarchy. One interviewee even noted that everything seemed to blend together. Beyond that, the image behind the text also decreased visual recognization and there is little distinction between one section of text to the other. Overall, the organization of element on this screen created a somewhat cluttered screen. I worked to address the issues mentioned above by increasing the opacity of the text section. I also added a header to the top of screen for continuity with the other screens and for easy visibility. Additional, I separated the paragraphical blurb and the ‚Äútry this‚Äù suggestion.

REFLECTION SCREEN

Tappings on any reflection row will lead users to the reflection page, where they can edit what they‚Äôve entered and add more if they wanted to. On the diary page, a user mentioned that they were not sure at first if any of the rows were click-able. Another noted that the bottom menu ‚Äúbubbles‚Äù around the icons were hard to see against the background. I decided to separate the entry rows a bit more to show distinction, adding an arrow to let the user know that they are accessible. I also added a button to add an entry by the Diary header.

CASE STUDY

FridgeShot

Capturing what‚Äôs in your fridge and maximizing its usage via generated, relevant recipes.

PRODUCT TYPE

A mobile app

CLIENT

Interview Study

PROJECT DATE

August 2020 - December 2020

MY ROLE

User Research, Concept Ideation, Visual design, Prototyping & testing, Flow and Interactions
Team: 4 designers and user researchers

SUMMARY

FridgeShot is an iOS application that can aid users by helping them create meals using only or primarily the ingredients they have readily available in their refrigerator. Using a Machine Learning algorithm which recognizes ingredients from an image, a user can simply snap a picture of their fridge and quickly view a generated list of its contents and recipes which use them.

THE CHALLENGE

We focused on solutions to limit space and/or difficulty in navigating cooking environments to provide Cornell undergraduates living off-campus without a meal plan with easier, quicker, and more comfortable cooking experiences as well as shorter and less frequent trips to the supermarket. FridgeShot is a mobile application that captures an image of the user‚Äôs fridge and uses image recognition to discern their fridge contents and suggest relevant recipes.

Our user demographic was Cornell undergraduates who aren‚Äôt on the campus meal-plan and cook in their off-campus residences. This app needed to help users maximize the usage of their ingredients, save time and money spent on trips to the store, and encourage the exploration of a diverse and balanced diet

KEY FOCUS AREAS

How long does it typically take users to prep and cook a meal?

What do users keep in mind when they‚Äôre cooking?

How often do users cook at home?

How do users choose what to buy and when?


SOLUTION

‚ÄúI get my groceries from Walmart‚Äù

‚ÄúI like to optimize the yield when I cook, reusing ingredients.‚Äù

‚ÄúI cook 4-5 times a week‚Äù

‚ÄúI buy 1-2 vegetables each trip to avoid waste‚Äù

‚ÄúI decide what to cook based on my oldest ingredient‚Äù

Then based on our found core needs we came up with a persona based on our insights to serve as a cumulative representation of all the common user needs, goals, tasks from user interviews.



IDEATION

The most common flaw with technologies that increase efficiency in cooking and cleaning is that it only applies to one part of the process, whether it‚Äôs making eggs with the Dash Egg Cooker, or scrubbing the counters with the Rubbermaid Power Scrubber. The processes these technologies tackle are niche and might not save too much time when looking at the bigger picture.

Our final idea was an app which scans a photo of your fridge contents, and suggests recipes based on your available ingredients. Our chosen design covers maximizing ingredient choices, meal prep, decreasing shopping frequency, and cook time. This app allows users to favorite recipes to use again. Its dependency on your current ingredients lets users easily grab what is already there to start making their meal, reducing the amount of time a user might spend staring at what they have and thinking about what they can make. Users can also plan ahead for future meals the app helps them get ideas for.



PROTOTYPING DECISIONS

Since our design is an iOS application, we formatted by prototype to fit a typical iphone in order to demonstrate the vast majority of what its realistic implementation would look like. The UI elements within the prototype are all intuitive or easy to learn, the bottom navigation bar really allows a user to navigate through all of the application and get a sense of its functionality. We got this prototype in front of people in a usability testing session, where we asked our test subjects to complete a set of tasks. We measured the outcome of these tasks based on the following metrics.

Usability: The user is able to complete the tasks with minimal error and does so along an optimal or near optimal path.

User Experience: The user does not get frustrated with the application and feels that they would actually use the product in their everyday life For this test, we used figma to prototype full flows of our concepts and interactions. From here, we evaluated and analyzed our results, and made our design changes, creating our high fidelity prototype.


DESIGN RATIONALE

Home: The home page displays data from the last fridge shot the user took. The inspiration for this is the stats based home page of fitness apps and saves the user‚Äôs last FridgeShot so that they can check for recipes again at a later time. Based on user feedback from our user tests, we rearranged the item listings in alphabetical order, so the user has an easier time checking whether they have a certain ingredient.

FridgeShot Camera + Add/Remove Ingredients: The camera app features a standard one click to capture. After analyzing the picture taken, FridgeShot categorizes scanned items into ‚ÄúScanned Ingredients‚Äù and ‚ÄúAdd to List.‚Äù The ingredients list features items identified by the algorithm at an optimal level of accuracy. The ‚ÄúAdd to List‚Äù category contains items, such as those that are partially covered, that are identifiable to a much lower level of accuracy.

Browsing recipes: Users can go to the browse screen to obtain the results from their FridgeShot. A user not only searches for recipes based on their fridge contents but also plan their shopping list with minimal wastage based on recipes that they already have most of the ingredients for

Favorites: The favorites page, visually similar to the browse page, allows users to return to recipes for quick access, save ones they liked, and easily find recipes without going through the entire process of taking a picture of one‚Äôs fridge and browsing recipes again.

Selected Recipe: The selected recipe page was designed to help users see not only the ingredients, but a step-by-step guide for the creation of the chosen recipe. This page can be found from the selection of a recipe from the browse page, and has a layout similar to just about any cooking site or app for increased user familiarity.

Account: The account page was created to give users a place to enter their app settings, notification settings and to access any support they may need.

RESULTS + LEARNINGS

Embrace design consistency: We learned that it is important for both visual mapping and user satisfaction that every page of our prototype felt and appeared to fit together.

Considering icon choices: It is important to use standard symbols for their respective functions, so that you can work with the user, not against them.

User Testing is Irreplacable: Our usability test was insightful and informed us of a few areas in which users struggled to complete tasks along the optimal path, as well as a few instances of user frustration.

Next Steps: We were made aware of a few things that went beyond our Figma prototype‚Äôs capabilities, such as issues adding and removing any given item from the scanned ingredients list, maintaining a favorited state on any given item across all pages, an inability to see if any given item is already entered into the ingredients list, and an inability to filter recipes in our browse page. Our metrics were extremely helpful in raising these issues even though they go beyond what is feasible at this current time for our prototype, which is still in early development and has yet to be given true functional implementation.
